Temperature Controlled Shipping - Guide to Reefer, Cold Chain & Refrigerated Freight

Temperature controlled shipping is one of the most unforgiving segments of freight logistics. One broken cold chain link can destroy an entire load. A one-degree temperature spike at the wrong moment can transform a $50,000 pharmaceutical shipment into worthless product. A delayed handoff between carriers can leave fruit rotting in a trailer. There’s no room for error here.

Yet every single day, perishable goods worth billions of dollars move across North America in refrigerated trucks, insulated containers, and temperature-controlled warehouses. Vaccines that save lives. Fresh produce that feeds families. Specialty chemicals that keep manufacturing plants running. The cold chain doesn’t just move products. It moves critical infrastructure for entire industries.

This guide walks you through everything you need to know about temperature controlled shipping: how it works, what it costs, where the risks hide, and how to avoid catastrophic failures.

What is Temperature Controlled Shipping?

Temperature controlled shipping is the transportation of goods that require specific temperature ranges to maintain quality, safety, or efficacy. Unlike standard dry freight, temperature controlled shipments demand active refrigeration, continuous monitoring, and precise compliance with regulatory standards. This includes refrigerated shipping and perishable shipping solutions for products from pharmaceuticals to fresh produce.

Why does this matter? Some products have narrow acceptable temperature ranges. Pharmaceuticals might degrade above 77°F. Frozen food can begin thawing at 0°F. Certain chemicals become unstable if they drop below 50°F. Once these thresholds are crossed, the product is compromised, unusable, or potentially dangerous.

Industries that depend on temperature controlled shipping include:

  • Pharmaceutical and biotechnology companies (vaccines, biologics, specialty medications)
  • Food and beverage distributors (fresh produce, dairy, meat, frozen goods)
  • Chemical manufacturers (specialty chemicals, adhesives, coatings)
  • Floral distributors (cut flowers, plants, arrangements)
  • Cosmetics and personal care brands (skincare serums, lipsticks, fragrances)
  • Nutraceutical companies (supplements, protein powders, amino acids)

The cost premium is steep, but the alternative is accepting total product loss. Most companies don’t have that choice.

Types of Temperature Controlled Equipment

Temperature controlled shipping isn’t one-size-fits-all. Different shipments require different equipment based on product type, distance, duration, and temperature sensitivity.

Reefer Trucks and Trailers

These are the backbone of temperature controlled freight. A reefer (refrigerated) truck is a semi-trailer equipped with a mechanical refrigeration unit powered by the truck’s engine or an auxiliary diesel engine. The refrigeration unit sits above the cab and circulates cooled air through the cargo space. Reefer trailers are the most common solution for full truckload shipments of temperature sensitive goods.

Modern reefer units maintain precise temperatures within one-degree ranges. They include digital temperature monitoring systems, backup power capabilities, and automated alarm systems if temperatures drift outside acceptable limits.

Insulated Containers and Thermally Protected Packaging

For smaller shipments or partial loads, insulated containers with gel packs, dry ice, or liquid nitrogen are standard. These passive cooling systems work for shipments lasting 24–72 hours. The thermal mass of the cooling agent combined with insulation slows temperature change.

These solutions are cheaper than reefer trucks but they don’t actively maintain temperature. They delay inevitable warming. A gel pack system is appropriate for an overnight pharmaceutical shipment. It’s inappropriate for a three-day journey across the country.

Refrigerated LTL (Less Than Truckload)

When you don’t need a full reefer truck, refrigerated LTL services allow you to share truck space and costs with other shippers. The entire trailer maintains a single temperature zone. This limits flexibility but reduces your per-pound shipping cost significantly. Learn more about what is LTL shipping and how it compares to full truckload alternatives.

Dry Ice Shipments

Dry ice (solid CO2) provides extremely cold temperatures (negative 109°F). It’s used for ultra-cold products like biological samples, specialty vaccines, and frozen proteins. Dry ice sublimates during transit, meaning it gradually disappears. You must factor in the sublimation rate when calculating how much dry ice to use for a shipment duration.

Gel Packs and Phase-Change Materials

Reusable gel packs and phase-change materials (like ice packs) absorb heat as they melt. They’re cost-effective for short-haul shipments. They’re useless for anything longer than 48 hours.

How Reefer Trucks Work

Understanding how reefer units operate helps explain why proper maintenance and pre-cooling matter so much.

The mechanical refrigeration system in a reefer trailer uses a compressor-based cooling cycle similar to a home air conditioner. The compressor is powered by an auxiliary diesel engine that runs independently from the tractor engine. This independent power supply means the refrigeration unit can operate whether the truck is moving or sitting in a loading dock.

The cooling cycle works like this. Refrigerant gas is compressed, cooling as it condenses into liquid. That liquid passes through an expansion device and evaporates in the trailer’s evaporator coil. As it evaporates, the refrigerant absorbs heat from the cargo space, cooling the air. A circulation fan distributes the cold air throughout the trailer. The refrigerant returns to the compressor to repeat the cycle.

Pre-Cooling: Non-Negotiable

Here’s where shippers often fail. A reefer unit maintains temperature. It doesn’t create temperature from ambient air. If you load 60,000 pounds of warm cargo into a trailer at 70°F, the refrigeration unit will spend hours pulling that cargo down to 33°F.

Pre-cooling means running the reefer unit in the empty trailer for 30–60 minutes before loading to bring the interior to target temperature. Many shippers skip this step to save time. This is a mistake. Under-cooled cargo arrives warm and can exceed acceptable temperature ranges during the first half of transit.

We recommend full reefer pre-cooling for any shipment above $10,000 in product value.

Temperature Monitoring and Documentation

Modern reefers include digital temperature sensors that record readings every 15–30 minutes. These data loggers are critical for regulatory compliance and for identifying when temperature excursions occurred. A temperature data log from the reefer unit is legal proof that your shipment maintained acceptable conditions.

Some reefer units include GPS and real-time monitoring, allowing shippers to track not just location but also temperature throughout transit. This visibility is essential for high-value shipments.

Fuel Consumption and Operating Costs

The auxiliary diesel engine powering the reefer unit consumes fuel constantly, whether the truck is moving at highway speeds or idling at a dock. Reefer fuel consumption ranges from 0.5 to 1.0 gallon per hour depending on outside temperature and how hard the refrigeration unit is working.

In summer, when outside temperatures are 90°F or higher, the reefer works harder and burns more fuel. A cross-country reefer shipment in July costs significantly more than the same shipment in March.

Temperature Zones and What Ships Where

Different products require different temperature ranges. Understanding temperature zones helps you select the right equipment and identify compatibility issues.

The critical detail is that you cannot mix temperature zones in a single reefer load. If you have frozen product that must stay below 0°F and refrigerated product that must stay above 33°F, you need two separate reefer trucks or a multi-temperature reefer unit (which are expensive and rare).

One temperature-sensitive product in a trailer dictates the temperature for the entire load. The trailer cools to that product’s requirement or the shipment fails.

Cold Chain Logistics Explained

The cold chain is the unbroken sequence of temperature-controlled environments from the point of origin to the final destination. Every handoff, every waiting period, every facility transition is a potential failure point.

How the Cold Chain Works

A typical cold chain looks like this. A pharmaceutical manufacturer produces temperature-sensitive medications in a temperature-controlled facility. The product is packed in insulated boxes and loaded into a refrigerated truck at a temperature-controlled warehouse. The reefer truck travels to a regional distribution hub, where the product is unloaded and transferred to temperature-controlled storage.

From there, the product might be consolidated with other shipments into a larger reefer truck destined for a national distributor. At the distributor’s facility, the product moves to climate-controlled storage. Finally, it’s loaded onto a final-mile reefer truck heading to a pharmacy or hospital.

Every single step requires proper temperature management. Every handoff is a risk point.

Handoff Points: Where Cold Chains Break

The most common cold chain failures occur during transfers between carriers or facilities. A shipment arrives at a dock at 2 AM but isn’t unloaded until 10 AM. The reefer truck’s engine is turned off during loading. The product sits in ambient air for eight hours.

Or a driver delivers a pharmaceutical shipment to a warehouse dock. The facility’s receiving department is overwhelmed. The reefer trailer sits idle for three hours before being connected to the facility’s powered dock infrastructure. That’s three hours of heat gain in an unrefrigerated trailer.

These scenarios happen constantly in logistics. Most shippers don’t track them. Most carriers don’t report them. The product arrives and passes visual inspection, so nobody knows the cold chain was broken.

Risk Areas in Temperature Controlled Shipping

Critical risk points include:

  • Loading delays (warm cargo loaded into unpre-cooled trailers)
  • Transit delays (traffic, breakdowns, waiting at receivers)
  • Dock transfers (time spent between unloading and next leg)
  • Facility storage (unrefrigerated warehouses, improper climate control)
  • Driver stops and breaks (reefer engine turned off to save fuel)
  • Seasonal extremes (extreme heat forcing reefer to work beyond capacity)

Any single point of failure can compromise the entire shipment.

Costs of Temperature Controlled Shipping

Temperature controlled shipping costs significantly more than standard dry freight. Understanding where that cost premium comes from helps you make informed decisions about when full reefer service is justified.

Base Rate Premiums

Standard LTL (less than truckload) freight costs approximately $1.50–$3.00 per pound depending on distance, weight, and freight class. Refrigerated LTL freight costs $2.50–$5.00 per pound. That’s a 50–100% premium for temperature control.

Full reefer truckload rates range from $4,000–$8,000 per shipment depending on distance, season, and current market demand. A standard full truckload might cost $1,200–$2,500 for the same lane.

Fuel Surcharges and Operating Costs

Reefer trucks burn more fuel because the auxiliary refrigeration engine runs continuously. Carriers add a fuel surcharge of 15–25% to reefer rates. During periods of high fuel prices, this surcharge increases.

In addition to fuel, reefer trailers require more maintenance. The refrigeration unit needs regular servicing. Compressors wear out. Evaporator coils freeze or corrode. These costs are built into carrier rates.

Pre-Cooling Charges

Some carriers charge separately for pre-cooling ($50–$150 per trailer). Others include it in the base rate. Always confirm pre-cooling procedures with your carrier in advance. Pre-cooling is one of several accessorial charges you may encounter in temperature controlled shipping.

Peak Season Premiums

During peak seasons (summer for frozen goods, holidays for fresh food, vaccine season for pharmaceuticals), reefer capacity is scarce. Rates increase 20–40%. In some markets, reefer trucks are simply unavailable at any price.

Real-World Cost Comparison

A 2,000-pound shipment of fresh produce from California to New York:

  • Dry freight LTL: $400–$600
  • Refrigerated LTL: $900–$1,400
  • Full reefer TL (if sharing with other shippers): $3,500–$5,000

A 15,000-pound pharmaceutical shipment requiring dedicated reefer service with temperature monitoring:

  • Refrigerated LTL (shared trailer): $4,500–$6,500
  • Full reefer TL (dedicated, pre-cooled): $6,500–$9,000
  • Full reefer TL with real-time temperature monitoring and specialized packing: $8,000–$12,000

The cheapest option isn’t always viable. A shared refrigerated LTL trailer might stop at five different facilities, exposing your product to repeated temperature fluctuations. A dedicated reefer eliminates those handoffs but costs more.

The right choice depends on your product value, temperature sensitivity, and acceptable risk level.

LTL vs. FTL for Refrigerated Freight

When you don’t need a full reefer truck, the decision between refrigerated LTL and full truckload becomes critical. Understanding LTL vs FTL shipping helps you select the right solution for your temperature-sensitive goods.

Refrigerated LTL: When to Use It

Refrigerated LTL (less than truckload) service consolidates shipments from multiple shippers into a single reefer trailer. You pay only for the space you use. This reduces your per-pound cost significantly.

Refrigerated LTL works when:

  • Your shipment is under 10,000 pounds
  • Your product’s temperature zone matches other shipments in the load
  • You can accept standard transit times (3–7 days)
  • Product value doesn’t justify dedicated reefer costs
  • You’re shipping non-emergency, non-perishable temperature-sensitive goods

The downside is lack of control. Your shipment travels with other products. It may stop at multiple facilities. It might wait in a consolidation warehouse for 12–24 hours. Those delays and handoffs increase temperature risk.

For vitamins or cosmetics, this risk is acceptable. For vaccines or fresh-caught seafood, this level of risk is unacceptable.

Full Truckload Reefer: When You Need It

Full truckload (FTL) reefer service means you get a dedicated reefer trailer. Your product travels directly to destination with no stops, no consolidations, no shared space.

Full reefer FTL is necessary when:

  • Your shipment exceeds 10,000 pounds (usually the breakeven point)
  • Your product has strict temperature requirements
  • Product value exceeds $10,000 (total loss risk is substantial)
  • You’re shipping perishable goods with short shelf life (fish, berries, meat)
  • You need guaranteed transit times and minimal handling
  • Regulatory requirements mandate dedicated transport

Full reefer eliminates consolidation delays and reduces handoff points. Your shipment maintains a consistent cold chain from loading to delivery.

The Breakeven Analysis

The decision point typically arrives at 8,000–10,000 pounds. Below this weight, refrigerated LTL is cheaper per pound. Above this weight, full reefer becomes cost-competitive because you’re paying for a dedicated trailer regardless of how full it is.

Calculate the total landed cost for your shipment using both methods. Add the cost of potential temperature excursions (product loss risk) and regulatory penalties into the calculation. For high-value products, full reefer often costs less when you account for risk.

Compliance and Regulations

Temperature controlled shipping isn’t just a logistics challenge. It’s a heavily regulated industry with serious legal consequences for violations.

FDA FSMA Compliance

The FDA Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) requires strict temperature control and documentation for food products moving through commerce. FSMA requires:

  • Adequate temperature records showing continuous maintenance of required temperatures
  • Documentation of all handoffs and transfers between facilities
  • Identification of responsible parties at each step of the supply chain
  • Investigation and corrective action procedures if temperatures exceed acceptable ranges

FSMA violations can result in product seizures, facility shutdowns, and significant civil penalties.

USDA Requirements for Meat and Poultry

The USDA enforces strict temperature controls for meat, poultry, and dairy products. These products must maintain specific temperature ranges throughout transport and storage. USDA inspectors audit carrier temperature records and can halt shipments if documentation is inadequate.

Pharmaceutical and Biologic Compliance

Pharmaceutical shipments fall under FDA regulation and often under additional regulations based on the specific product (controlled substances, for example). Temperature stability data must be maintained for the entire supply chain. If temperature excursions occur, manufacturers must complete stability studies to determine if the product remains safe and effective.

High-value pharmaceutical shipments may require:

  • GPS tracking and real-time temperature monitoring
  • Chain of custody documentation signed at every handoff
  • Specialized reefer equipment meeting pharmaceutical industry standards
  • Carrier qualification and audit programs
  • Insurance policies specifically covering temperature-sensitive goods

Documentation Matters More Than You Think

Here’s the critical reality: temperature data is legal evidence. If a refrigerated shipment arrives at a destination and someone claims it was mishandled, the temperature data log proves whether that’s true. If your reefer unit wasn’t equipped to record temperatures, you have no proof of compliance.

Shippers who skip temperature monitoring because it’s inconvenient create legal liability. When a temperature-sensitive shipment fails, the first question is “where’s the temperature documentation?” If you don’t have it, you assume full liability regardless of what actually happened.

Common Temperature Controlled Shipping Mistakes

Experienced logistics professionals can spot these mistakes instantly. Newer shippers often learn them the hard way by losing product.

Pre-Cool Failures

Loading warm cargo into an unpre-cooled reefer trailer is the single most common mistake. The refrigeration unit will eventually cool the cargo, but it takes hours. During those hours, the cargo is warming. For products with tight temperature tolerances, this is unacceptable.

We’ve seen pharmaceutical companies lose $50,000 vaccine shipments because the trailer wasn’t pre-cooled and the cargo reached 80°F before cooling began.

Mixed-Temperature Loads

Attempting to ship products with different temperature requirements in the same reefer trailer creates impossible conditions. A trailer can maintain one temperature. If your load requires both frozen and refrigerated zones, you need two trailers.

Many shippers discover this problem when a truck arrives at a dock and the receiver refuses to accept the mixed-temperature shipment. Now your product has wasted transit time and costs will increase significantly.

Broken Temperature Monitoring

Carriers sometimes disable or fail to properly maintain temperature monitoring systems. Digital data loggers fail. Sensors drift out of calibration. Carriers that don’t invest in monitoring tend to be the ones that cause problems.

Always confirm that your specific shipment has a functioning temperature recording device. Request that the device be tested before loading.

Inadequate Packaging

Product packaging affects how quickly temperature changes propagate through a shipment. Thin packaging provides minimal insulation. If your product is vulnerable to temperature fluctuations and you’re relying on passive cooling (gel packs), packaging quality matters enormously.

Inadequate packaging means your temperature safeguards fail faster than you planned.

Ignoring Seasonal Extremes

Ambient temperature affects refrigeration unit performance. On a 95°F day, a reefer unit works harder and maintains less consistent internal temperatures. On a negative-10°F day, the evaporator coils may freeze if the controller isn’t calibrated for cold climates.

Seasonal routing decisions can make or break a shipment. A summer reefer route through the southwest is riskier than a spring route through Canada, even though the southwest route is shorter.

Carrier Selection Based on Price Alone

Reefer freight isn’t commoditized. Different carriers operate at different quality levels. Some maintain modern reefer equipment with current monitoring systems. Others operate aging trailers where temperature control is inconsistent.

The cheapest carrier is never the right call for cold chain. Pay for carriers with proven track records, modern equipment, and transparent temperature monitoring.

Best Practices for Shippers

Successful temperature controlled shipping requires discipline and planning. These best practices significantly reduce the risk of cold chain failures.

Plan for Pre-Cooling

Schedule loading times that allow for adequate pre-cooling. 60 minutes minimum for most reefer trailers, longer for large loads or in extreme temperatures. Communicate pre-cooling requirements to your carrier in advance.

Implement Continuous Monitoring

For shipments above $10,000 in value, invest in real-time temperature monitoring systems. GPS tracking combined with temperature data provides end-to-end visibility. Shippers can identify problems while the truck is still in transit, allowing for corrective action.

Establish Backup Plans

What happens if the reefer unit fails mid-transit? What happens if a truck breaks down and your product sits in ambient air for four hours? Develop contingency plans before emergencies occur.

These plans might include carrier backup lists, insurance coverage verification, and pre-arranged transfer procedures if your shipment needs to move to another truck.

Vet Your Carriers Thoroughly

Don’t ship temperature-sensitive goods with carriers you haven’t researched. Request references from other shippers using their reefer fleet. Ask to inspect reefer equipment. Confirm their temperature monitoring systems. Request their incident reports and failure rates.

Spend time on carrier selection. It pays dividends in avoided product losses.

Invest in Proper Packaging

Packaging is your product’s first line of defense against temperature fluctuations. Invest in quality insulated boxes, appropriate cooling media (gel packs, dry ice, phase-change materials), and quality thermal protection.

Poor packaging is false economy. The cost of superior packaging is trivial compared to the cost of product loss.

Document Everything

Maintain detailed records of every shipment. Record pre-cooling times, loading temperatures, carrier information, route taken, and delivery conditions. Photograph temperature monitoring devices before loading and at delivery.

This documentation protects you legally and helps identify patterns if problems occur. You’ll be able to spot which carriers, routes, or conditions create higher risk.

Communicate Clearly with Receivers

The receiving dock is a critical handoff point. Communicate loading temperatures, expected arrival conditions, and required storage procedures to your receiver in advance. Ensure they understand that the product must be unloaded and stored properly immediately upon arrival.

A receiver who leaves a refrigerated shipment on a warm dock for two hours can destroy your entire load in the final step of the journey.

Industries and Specific Use Cases

Different industries face distinct temperature control challenges. Understanding industry-specific requirements helps explain why some shipments warrant premium cold chain investments.

Pharmaceutical and Biotechnology

Vaccines, biologics, and specialty medications often require consistent storage between 35–46°F. Excursions beyond this range can render products ineffective or dangerous. Pharmaceutical companies use specialized carriers with validated reefer equipment and real-time monitoring.

A $50,000 shipment of specialty cancer medications demands full reefer with dedicated carrier and continuous monitoring. No shortcuts.

Fresh Produce and Food Distribution

Fresh fruits and vegetables are living organisms. They continue respiring during transport, consuming oxygen and producing ethylene gas. Temperature control slows respiration and extends shelf life, but it doesn’t stop it.

Berries might have a three-day shelf life. Apples might have three weeks. Carrots might have two months. Reefer temperature and ventilation must be optimized for each product category. A single temperature doesn’t work for mixed fruit loads.

Frozen Foods

Frozen foods must remain below 0°F to prevent thawing and ice crystal formation. Ice cream requires ultra-cold transport (below -10°F). Frozen prepared meals tolerate slightly warmer freezer temperatures (0 to -5°F).

Frozen food shipments are often full reefer truckloads because product volume is high and temperature consistency is critical.

Floral Distribution

Cut flowers are extremely time-sensitive and require consistent cool temperatures (33–40°F) and specific humidity levels. Broken cold chains in floral distribution mean a flower grower loses an entire crop that took months to produce.

Floral logistics demands fast, dedicated reefer service with minimal handling delays.

Cosmetics and Personal Care

Many cosmetics contain temperature-sensitive ingredients. Certain serums can crystallize if frozen. Fragrances can separate if heated. Lip products can melt. While cosmetics are more forgiving than pharmaceuticals, they still demand temperature control.

Most cosmetics ship in controlled room temperature (59–77°F) rather than refrigerated conditions. Insulated containers with gel packs usually suffice for short-haul shipments.

Chemical Manufacturers

Specialty chemicals often have specific temperature requirements for stability and performance. Some adhesives gel if too cold. Some coatings become too thick if too warm. Volatile chemicals require temperature control to prevent pressure buildup.

Chemical shipments demand carrier expertise and proper equipment classification. Not all reefers are equipped to safely transport certain chemicals.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a reefer truck and how does it differ from a standard refrigerated trailer?

A reefer truck is a semi-trailer equipped with an independent refrigeration unit powered by an auxiliary diesel engine. The refrigeration unit maintains specific temperatures throughout transit regardless of outside temperatures. This differs from passive cooling methods (gel packs, insulation) which only slow temperature change without actively cooling.

How much does temperature controlled shipping cost compared to standard freight?

Temperature controlled shipping costs 50–200% more than standard dry freight depending on equipment type and distance. Refrigerated LTL costs roughly 50–100% more per pound than dry LTL. Full reefer truckload service typically costs 200–400% more than equivalent dry freight service, but the cost difference decreases for large shipments where the full truck is nearly full.

What temperature ranges are required for different products?

Temperature requirements vary by product. Frozen foods require below 0°F. Refrigerated products typically require 33–40°F. Many pharmaceuticals require 36–46°F. Controlled room temperature products require 59–77°F. Always verify specific temperature requirements with the product manufacturer or your regulatory compliance team.

How long can gel packs keep products cold during shipping?

Gel packs maintain cold temperatures for 24–48 hours depending on outside temperature, package insulation quality, and the amount of cooling media used. In extreme heat or with minimal insulation, effectiveness decreases to 12–18 hours. For shipments longer than 48 hours, active refrigeration (reefer truck) is necessary.

What happens if a temperature excursion occurs during shipping?

The consequences depend on the product and the severity of the excursion. Some pharmaceuticals remain viable after brief temperature excursions. Others are immediately compromised. Frozen foods may thaw partially. Fresh produce may accelerate ripening or spoilage. You must contact the product manufacturer or your quality assurance team to determine if the product remains acceptable.

Is real-time temperature monitoring necessary for all shipments?

Real-time monitoring is essential for high-value shipments (above $10,000), pharmaceutical products requiring regulatory documentation, and any shipment where early detection of temperature problems could enable corrective action. For lower-value shipments or less temperature-sensitive products, temperature data loggers that record readings every 15–30 minutes are usually sufficient.

How do I choose between refrigerated LTL and full reefer truckload service?

Use refrigerated LTL for shipments under 8,000–10,000 pounds where cost is the primary concern and modest temperature fluctuations are acceptable. Use full reefer truckload for shipments over 10,000 pounds, high-value products, extremely temperature-sensitive goods, or when you need guaranteed transit times and minimal handling.

What regulations apply to temperature controlled shipping?

FDA FSMA regulations apply to food products. USDA regulations apply to meat, poultry, and dairy. FDA regulations apply to pharmaceuticals and biologics. Specific products may have additional regulatory requirements. Always verify applicable regulations with your compliance team and your carrier’s regulatory expertise.

Can I mix different products with different temperature requirements in one reefer trailer?

No. A reefer maintains a single temperature throughout the trailer. If your products require different temperature zones, you need separate trailers. Attempting to ship mixed-temperature products in one trailer either leaves some products outside their acceptable temperature range or requires compromise temperatures that may be inadequate for both products.

What should I do if a temperature-sensitive shipment is delayed or experiences problems in transit?

Contact your carrier immediately and request that the reefer temperature be increased slightly above normal operating range to reduce cold shock to the product. Request temperature data from the reefer unit. Contact the product manufacturer’s technical team with the temperature history to determine if the product remains acceptable. Document everything and notify your insurance carrier if the product is a total loss.

Conclusion

Temperature controlled shipping is complex, expensive, and unforgiving. One mistake can destroy an entire load. One temperature spike can render months of production work worthless. One handoff delay can spoil products with tight shelf lives.

Yet thousands of companies successfully ship temperature-sensitive goods every day. They do it by treating cold chain seriously, investing in proper carriers and equipment, implementing continuous monitoring, and maintaining meticulous documentation.

The choice between cheapest and best isn’t always clear. But for temperature-sensitive freight, the equation is simple: the cheapest carrier is rarely the right choice, and skipping proper refrigeration always costs more than skipping the investment in premium service.
