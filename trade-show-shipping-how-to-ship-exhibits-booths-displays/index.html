Trade Show Shipping: How to Ship Exhibits, Booths & Displays

Your company spent $50,000 on a custom exhibit. The graphics are flawless. The demo stations are wired. The sales team is booked on flights.

None of it matters if the booth arrives a day late or in pieces.

Trade show shipping is not a freight problem. It is a deadline-risk management problem. The cargo is expensive, but the real cost of failure is everything that happens after the freight fails: empty booth space, missed meetings, wasted sponsorship dollars, lost pipeline, and a team standing around with nothing to show. A $1,500 shipping cost decision can trigger $50,000 or more in downstream business losses.

The cheapest carrier is irrelevant if your booth arrives late or damaged. Trade show freight is one of the rare situations where saving $300 on a rate can cost you the entire ROI of the event.

This guide covers planning timelines, carrier options, advance warehouse logistics, costs, packaging, documentation, return shipping, contingency planning, and the mistakes that cost exhibitors real money every year.

What Makes Trade Show Shipping Different

Treating exhibit shipping like a normal shipment is the fastest way to end up standing in an empty booth space on opening morning. Three things make convention shipping fundamentally different.

Deadlines Are Absolute

Venues set hard receiving windows, sometimes as narrow as 48 hours before the show opens. Miss the window, and your shipment goes into storage at the carrier’s terminal. Sometimes the venue refuses late deliveries entirely.

The show opens when the show opens. There is no extension.

Venues Have Strict Rules

Convention centers operate under labor agreements, union rules, and facility-specific regulations that dictate how freight is received, moved, and placed. Every venue publishes an exhibitor kit with material handling rules. Ignore them, and your freight sits on a loading dock.

The Cargo Is Fragile and Expensive

Trade show materials are built for visual impact, not for the abuse of a freight trailer. LED walls, printed fabric displays, product samples, and custom cabinetry all travel in the same shipment. A cracked monitor panel cannot be replaced overnight in Las Vegas.

The True Cost of a Late or Damaged Booth Shipment

The freight charge is the smallest number in the equation. Here is what a late booth shipment actually costs when you account for everything beyond the freight invoice.

Example: A mid-size B2B software company ships a 20x20 island booth to a major industry conference in Chicago. The LTL carrier they selected (saving $400 over a specialized exhibit carrier) delivers the shipment one day after the advance warehouse deadline.

The $400 they saved on freight cost them over $12,000 in hard costs and an estimated $80,000 in sales pipeline. This scenario plays out at every major trade show, every year.

Start early. The number one cause of trade show shipping failures is starting too late. Here is the timeline experienced exhibitors follow.

Advance Warehouse vs. Direct-to-Show: A Countdown Comparison

The choice between advance warehouse and direct-to-show delivery comes down to how much schedule risk you are willing to accept.

Advance Warehouse Path (Recommended)

  • 4 weeks before show: Freight ships from your facility.
  • 3 weeks before show: Freight arrives at advance warehouse. Warehouse logs receipt, confirms piece count, and stores freight.
  • 2 weeks before show: You receive delivery confirmation. You have 2 full weeks of buffer before the show floor opens.
  • Setup day: Advance warehouse delivers freight to your booth space on schedule.

Direct-to-Show Path (Higher Risk)

  • 10 to 14 days before show: Freight ships from your facility.
  • 3 to 5 days before show: Freight must arrive during the venue’s narrow direct receiving window (often 48 to 72 hours).
  • If delayed: You have zero buffer. Late freight is refused or hit with significant surcharges. Your booth may not be ready when the show opens.

For anything more complex than a pop-up banner and a box of brochures, the advance warehouse path is the correct choice. The additional cost of warehouse handling is insurance against the catastrophic cost of late delivery.

Trade Show Shipping Options

Advance Warehouse Shipping

The advance warehouse is a facility operated by the show’s official general contractor (Freeman, GES, or Shepard). It opens weeks before the show to receive exhibitor freight. Your shipment arrives, gets logged into the system, and is delivered to your booth space at the designated setup time.

Ship to the advance warehouse. Always.

The warehouse acts as a buffer against transit delays. If your LTL shipment arrives three days early, the warehouse holds it. If your carrier runs a day behind, you still have margin before the show floor delivery window.

Direct to Venue

Some shows allow exhibitors to ship directly to the convention center during a narrow receiving window. This skips the advance warehouse costs. It also eliminates your safety margin entirely.

Direct shipping works for small, simple booths (a pop-up display, a box of brochures). For anything larger than what fits in the back of a van, the risk is not worth the savings.

Carrier Selection

For trade show freight, carrier experience matters more than price. Your options include:

  • Specialized exhibit carriers that focus exclusively on convention shipping
  • LTL carriers with trade show experience for smaller booths and standard displays
  • Full truckload carriers for large island booths, heavy machinery, or multi-crate exhibits
  • White glove delivery services for high-value electronics or anything needing setup assistance

Choose a carrier with trade show experience. The cheapest quote from a carrier that has never delivered to McCormick Place is not actually cheap. A carrier that has never navigated union labor rules, marshaling yard procedures, and show-specific receiving windows is a carrier that will cost you more than the rate difference when something goes wrong.

Proactive tracking matters here more than in almost any other freight scenario. You need to know where your exhibit is every day between pickup and delivery, with enough lead time to execute a backup plan if something goes sideways.

Contingency Planning: What to Do When Things Go Wrong

Every experienced trade show shipper has a backup plan. Here is what a solid contingency framework looks like.

Before the Show: Build Your Backup Plan

Identify local exhibit rental companies. For every show city, know at least one local exhibit rental company that can provide emergency replacement displays, furniture, monitors, and signage. Keep their contact information in your show binder and on your phone.

Carry critical items separately. Anything small enough to fly with you should fly with you. Product samples, USB drives with presentations, business cards, lead scanners, and printed collateral should travel in checked luggage or carry-on, not in the freight shipment. If your booth arrives and your marketing materials do not, you can still work the show. If your booth does not arrive but your team has product samples and a folding table, you can still have meetings.

Pre-authorize emergency spending. Decide in advance what you are willing to spend on emergency solutions. $2,000 for a rental display? $5,000 for same-day air freight? Having spending authority pre-approved eliminates decision paralysis during a crisis.

Contingency Plan Example

Scenario: Your 10x20 booth is scheduled to arrive at the advance warehouse on March 15 for a March 28 show in Las Vegas. On March 13, your carrier notifies you that the truck broke down in Texas and your shipment will be delayed by 3 days.

Response timeline:

  • March 13 (Day of notification): Contact the advance warehouse to confirm the late arrival will still be accepted (it will be, with a surcharge). Contact your expedited freight provider to assess whether re-routing to an expedited carrier can recover the schedule.
  • March 14: If expedited re-routing is not viable, confirm the late-arrival surcharge rate and accept it. Notify the show setup team that booth delivery to the floor may be delayed. Contact a Las Vegas exhibit rental company and put a backup 10x20 rental display on hold (cancelable within 48 hours).
  • March 16–17: Freight arrives at advance warehouse. Cancel the rental display hold. Proceed with standard setup.
  • If freight does not arrive by March 22: Activate the rental display. Ship critical items (product samples, monitors, printed materials) via expedited freight or air courier. The rental display plus your key materials allows you to work the show while the delayed freight is rerouted.

The cost of maintaining a contingency plan (a few phone calls and a cancelable hold) is negligible. The cost of not having one is an empty booth space.

Working with Advance Warehouses

Understanding how the advance warehouse works saves you money and headaches.

What the Advance Warehouse Does

The warehouse receives your freight, stores it, and delivers it to your booth location on the show floor. After the show, it picks up your freight, stores it, and ships it back to your facility or your next show. Every piece is tracked by booth number and exhibitor name.

Advance Warehouse Costs

You pay for the warehouse’s receiving, storage, and handling services on top of your transportation costs. Rates are charged per hundredweight (CWT), with minimums.

Expect to pay $80 to $130 per CWT for standard handling. There are discounts for freight that arrives before the early deadline and surcharges for late arrivals. The difference between “advance” and “direct” warehouse rates can be 30% or more.

Those costs are worth it. The advance warehouse is insurance against show week chaos.

Advance Warehouse Deadlines

Every show publishes two key deadlines:

  1. Advance warehouse receiving deadline. Freight must arrive by this date (usually 7 to 14 days before the show) to qualify for standard rates.
  2. Direct-to-venue receiving window. A narrow window (often 2 to 3 days) when freight can ship directly to the convention center.

Late arrivals at the advance warehouse get hit with overtime surcharges. Freight arriving at the venue outside the receiving window may be refused entirely.

Costs of Trade Show Shipping

Trade show freight costs more than standard freight. Budget accordingly.

Rate Factors

  • Weight and dimensions. A 10x10 inline booth might weigh 500 to 1,500 pounds. A 20x20 island booth can exceed 5,000 pounds.
  • Distance. Cross-country moves cost more than regional ones.
  • Carrier type. Specialized exhibit carriers charge a premium over standard LTL rates.
  • Advance warehouse handling. The general contractor’s drayage fees are a significant line item.
  • Timing. Rush shipments and late warehouse arrivals both carry surcharges.

Drayage at the Venue

Drayage is the cost of moving your freight from the loading dock to your booth space. The general contractor handles this, and you pay for it whether you like the rate or not. Rates vary by show but commonly fall between $80 and $160 per CWT. For a 2,000-pound booth, that is $1,600 to $3,200 just to get your crates to your booth space.

You cannot avoid drayage. Most convention centers require all freight to be handled by the official contractor.

Packaging Trade Show Materials

How you pack determines whether your exhibit arrives ready to set up or ready for the dumpster.

Crates vs. Cases

Custom wood crates are the standard for large exhibits, custom structures, and anything that will ship to multiple shows. A well-built crate protects your exhibit in transit and through the handling at the advance warehouse. Crates are reusable for years if maintained.

Molded plastic cases (Pelican, SKB, or similar) work well for smaller components, monitors, and accessories. They are lighter than wood crates, which reduces shipping and drayage costs.

Cardboard boxes are a last resort. They collapse under the weight of other freight in the trailer and signal to handlers that the contents are disposable. Fine for brochures. Not fine for anything you spent real money on.

Weight Distribution

Pack heavy items at the bottom of each crate. Distribute weight evenly so crates do not tip during transit. Use foam inserts, corner protectors, and internal bracing for fragile items. Pack every piece of electronics as if the crate will be dropped from three feet. At some point during its journey, it probably will be.

Labeling on Packaging

Every crate, case, and box needs labels on at least two sides: company name, booth number, show name and dates, piece count (e.g., “Piece 1 of 5”), weight, “FRAGILE” where applicable, and arrow indicators for orientation.

Missing labels cause lost freight. A crate with no booth number will sit in a warehouse until someone figures out where it belongs.

Documentation and Labeling

Proper paperwork separates a smooth trade show shipping experience from a disaster.

Material Handling Forms

The show’s general contractor requires a material handling form for every exhibitor receiving freight. This form tells the contractor what is coming, how many pieces, the total weight, and the expected arrival date. Submit it on time. Late or missing forms delay your booth delivery.

Target Labels

Target labels are the most important piece of paper in trade show freight. They go on every single piece and contain: exhibitor company name, booth number, show name and city, number of pieces in the shipment, and weight per piece.

The show’s exhibitor kit provides the target label template. Use it exactly as provided.

Bill of Lading

Your bill of lading must reference the advance warehouse address, your company name, booth number, and show name. If the BOL says “deliver to 123 Warehouse Blvd” without referencing the show or booth number, the warehouse cannot route your freight properly.

Include the show name, booth number, and piece count on the BOL. Every time.

Trade Show Shipping Checklist

Use this checklist before every shipment. Missing any item creates risk.

  • Target labels applied to every piece (at least 2 sides per crate/case)
  • Target labels include: company name, booth number, show name, city, piece count, weight
  • Bill of lading includes: advance warehouse address, company name, booth number, show name, piece count
  • Material handling form submitted to general contractor by deadline
  • Advance warehouse address and receiving hours confirmed
  • Show-site contact name and phone number documented on BOL and shared with carrier
  • Delivery window confirmed with advance warehouse or venue
  • Return shipping carrier and pickup date booked
  • Cargo insurance confirmed for full replacement value
  • Fragile items packed with foam, corner protectors, and internal bracing
  • Electronics packed in hard cases (Pelican, SKB, or custom crates)
  • Critical items (samples, presentations, lead scanners, business cards) packed separately for carry-on or checked luggage
  • Contingency plan documented: local rental company contact, expedited shipping provider, pre-authorized emergency budget
  • Photographic record of packed crate contents before sealing

Claim Documentation for Booth and Display Damage

Trade show freight takes more abuse than standard freight. The handling chain is longer: your facility to carrier, carrier to advance warehouse, warehouse to show floor, show floor back to warehouse, warehouse back to carrier, carrier back to your facility. Every handoff is an opportunity for damage.

What to Do When Damage Occurs

Document at the booth. When your freight arrives at your booth space and you discover damage, document it immediately. Do not wait until after setup. Do not wait until after the show. Photograph every piece of damage from multiple angles. Include wide shots that show the overall crate condition alongside close-ups of the specific damage.

Note damage on the delivery receipt. The general contractor’s delivery team will have you sign a receipt. Write “DAMAGED” and a brief description directly on the receipt before signing. If you sign a clean receipt and then discover damage later, your claim becomes much harder to win.

File a claim with the carrier promptly. Most carriers require claims to be filed within 9 months under the Carmack Amendment, but filing sooner strengthens your position. Include photographs, the delivery receipt with damage notation, the original bill of lading, proof of product value (purchase invoices, replacement cost estimates), and a written description of the damage and its impact.

Retain damaged materials. Do not discard damaged items until the claim is resolved. The carrier or insurer may request inspection. Disposing of damaged goods before inspection can void your claim.

For a detailed walkthrough of the freight claims process and how cargo insurance protects exhibit shipments, see our guide to cargo insurance and freight claims.

Return Shipping

Return shipping is where most exhibitors drop the ball.

The show ends. Your team is exhausted. Everyone wants to get to the airport. The booth needs to be broken down, repacked, labeled, and picked up by a carrier within a window that is usually just a few hours after the show floor closes.

Plan Return Shipping Before the Show

Do not wait until the last day of the show to figure out how your exhibit is getting home. Book your return shipment at the same time you book your outbound shipment. Confirm the carrier, the pickup date, and the destination.

Empty vs. Repack

If you brought crates, repack everything into the same crates it arrived in. Match every piece to its original case. Reassemble internal padding and bracing.

“Empty” return means the general contractor packs and ships your exhibit for you. It is expensive (often 2x to 3x the cost of a carrier you book yourself), and the packing quality is unpredictable.

Post-Show Logistics

The show floor becomes controlled chaos during teardown. Hundreds of exhibitors break down booths simultaneously while forklifts move freight to the loading dock.

Label every piece for return before you leave. Attach new target labels with your return address. Fill out the outbound material handling form and hand it to the general contractor’s service desk.

Walking away without completing return paperwork results in storage charges that accumulate daily until someone sorts it out.

Common Trade Show Shipping Mistakes

Every one of these is avoidable.

1. Shipping Too Late

The most common and most expensive mistake. Exhibitors wait until 10 days before the show to ship, the carrier hits a delay, and the booth misses the advance warehouse deadline. Now you are paying rush surcharges, overtime drayage, or scrambling for emergency air freight.

Ship four weeks before the show. Not two. Four.

2. Wrong or Missing Labels

A crate without a target label is a crate that gets lost. A crate with the wrong booth number gets delivered to someone else’s space. Label every piece, on multiple sides, with the correct show information.

3. Skipping Insurance

A mid-size SaaS company shipped a custom exhibit to a major tech conference in San Francisco. The crate took a hard hit in transit. The LED video wall cracked beyond repair. Total damage: $18,000. They had no cargo insurance.

That mistake cost them three days of leads.

Insure your exhibit for its full replacement value. The cost of trade show freight insurance is a fraction of what you stand to lose.

4. Ignoring Venue Rules

Every convention center has rules about receiving hours, vehicle restrictions, dock assignments, and labor requirements. Violations result in your shipment being turned away or held at the dock with penalty fees.

Read the exhibitor kit. Follow it exactly.

5. Not Booking Return Shipping in Advance

If you do not have a return carrier confirmed before the show starts, you are gambling with post-show chaos. Carriers fill up fast during major show teardowns. Wait too long and you are paying emergency rates or leaving your booth in a contractor’s warehouse at $50 per day in storage.

6. Underestimating Drayage Costs

Drayage surprises first-time exhibitors every single year. Budget for it explicitly. Do not treat it as a rounding error on your trade show budget.

7. Using the Wrong Carrier

Price-shopping for trade show freight the same way you shop for standard LTL is a recipe for problems. One missed delivery window costs more than the savings on the rate.

8. No Contingency Plan

Exhibitors who assume everything will go according to plan are the exhibitors who end up scrambling on setup day. Build a contingency plan before the show. Know your backup options. Pre-authorize emergency spending. The 30 minutes you spend planning for failure will save you thousands if things go wrong.

Frequently Asked Questions

How far in advance should I ship to a trade show?

Ship to the advance warehouse at least 3 to 4 weeks before the show opens. This provides a buffer for transit delays and ensures your freight arrives before the advance warehouse deadline. For shows with early-bird drayage discounts, shipping even earlier saves money on handling fees.

What is trade show drayage?

Drayage is the handling and transportation of your freight from the convention center’s loading dock to your booth space. The show’s general contractor manages drayage, charged per hundredweight (CWT). You cannot opt out at most venues.

Can I ship trade show materials myself using a personal vehicle?

Some shows allow hand-carry for materials that fit in a personal vehicle, usually under 200 pounds. Anything exceeding those limits must go through the official material handling process. Check the exhibitor kit for your show’s hand-carry policy.

How much does trade show shipping cost?

A small 10x10 inline booth might cost $800 to $2,500 round trip for transportation plus $400 to $1,200 in drayage. A 20x20 island booth can run $3,500 to $8,000 in shipping plus $2,000 to $5,000 in drayage. Get quotes early and budget for both transportation and venue handling fees.

What is an advance warehouse for trade shows?

A facility operated by the show’s general contractor that receives exhibitor freight weeks before the show. The warehouse stores your freight and delivers it to your booth space during setup. It costs more than direct-to-venue shipping but provides a critical buffer against transit delays.

Do I need insurance for trade show shipping?

Yes. Full stop. Standard carrier liability covers pennies on the dollar compared to the actual value of your exhibit. Cargo insurance protects you against damage, loss, and theft during transit.

What happens if my trade show shipment arrives late?

If your freight misses the advance warehouse deadline, you will pay late-arrival surcharges (often 30% to 50% above standard rates). If it misses the direct-to-venue window entirely, the venue may refuse delivery. Your options at that point are emergency courier services, local exhibit rental, or an empty booth space. None of those are cheap.

How do I handle return shipping after a trade show?

Book your return shipment before the show begins. During teardown, repack your exhibit into its original crates, apply new target labels with your return address, and submit the outbound material handling form. Have a team member stay through teardown to confirm the carrier pickup.

Ship Smart. Show Up Ready.

Plan early. Ship to the advance warehouse. Label everything correctly. Insure your freight. Book your return before the show opens. Build a contingency plan before you need one.

Trade show freight is deadline-risk management. The companies that show up ready are the companies that planned for what could go wrong, not just what should go right.
