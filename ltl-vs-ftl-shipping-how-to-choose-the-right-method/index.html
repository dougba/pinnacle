LTL vs FTL Shipping: How to Choose the Right Method

You've got freight to move. The first question every shipper faces: should you book less-than-truckload (LTL) or full truckload (FTL)?

The obvious answer is weight and pallet count. But that is the wrong starting point.

The real question is total cost, and total cost includes everything that happens after the rate is quoted: accessorial charges, reclassification fees, reweigh adjustments, damage claims, handling risk, and the operational burden of managing exceptions. A shipment that looks cheaper as LTL on the rate sheet can become the most expensive option on your invoice once carriers add reclass charges, limited-access fees, and damage replacement costs. A full truckload move that provides control and speed only delivers value if the carrier communicates proactively and holds to the quoted rate.

After years of helping shippers make this decision, we've seen the patterns clearly. Most shippers figure this out the hard way. This guide compares LTL vs FTL shipping across every factor that matters, including the hidden cost drivers most comparisons ignore, so you don't have to learn it the expensive way.

The Quick Answer: LTL vs FTL at a Glance

Factor LTL (Less-Than-Truckload) FTL (Full Truckload)
Shipment size 150-10,000 lbs / 1-10 pallets 10,000+ lbs / 10+ pallets
Trailer use Shared with other shippers Dedicated to your freight
Pricing Per hundredweight (CWT) + class Per mile or flat rate per load
Transit time 1-5+ days 1-3 days for comparable distance
Handling Multiple touchpoints (terminals/hubs) Minimal: origin to destination
Best for Smaller, regular shipments Large shipments, fragile goods, time-sensitive loads

If your freight fills more than half a trailer, FTL almost always wins on value. If it fills less than a quarter, LTL is the clear choice. The interesting decisions happen in between, and that's where smart shippers find their biggest savings.

What is LTL Shipping?

LTL (less-than-truckload) is a freight method where multiple shippers share space on the same trailer. Your pallets ride alongside freight from other businesses, and each shipper pays for their proportional share of the truck.

LTL carriers operate hub-and-spoke networks. Your freight gets picked up locally, taken to a terminal, sorted, consolidated with freight heading the same direction, transported via linehaul to a destination terminal, and finally delivered on a local truck. It's a complex system designed to maximize the utility of every inch.

The consolidation model makes LTL economical for smaller shipments. You pay only for the space your freight occupies, not for empty trailer capacity sitting unused.

For a deep dive into how LTL works, see our complete LTL shipping guide.

LTL works best when:

  • Your shipment weighs 150-10,000 lbs
  • You're shipping 1-10 pallets
  • Standard transit times (1-5 days) work for your timeline
  • Cost efficiency matters more than speed
  • You ship regularly in moderate quantities

When LTL Looks Cheaper but Becomes Expensive

The base LTL rate is almost always lower than FTL for shipments under 8,000 pounds. That is by design. The rate sheet does not include the costs that appear after pickup.

Reclassification. You ship a product at Class 85. The carrier inspects the freight at the terminal and determines the density supports Class 92.5. Your rate increases 15-25%. You dispute it. The dispute takes two weeks. You may or may not win.

Reweigh. You declare 4,200 pounds on the BOL. The carrier's terminal scale reads 4,680 pounds. The rate adjusts upward. If the weight crosses a rate break threshold, the increase is even larger.

Accessorials. Your consignee's dock is not accessible before 10 a.m. That is an appointment fee. The dock is shared with other tenants. That may trigger a limited-access fee. The shipment needs a liftgate at delivery because the receiving location does not have a dock. That is another $75-$150 on top of the base rate.

Handling damage. Your freight passes through four to six touchpoints between pickup and delivery. Each touchpoint is an opportunity for forklift damage, pallet shifting, or water exposure. A 2% damage rate across 200 annual LTL shipments means four damaged shipments per year. At $3,000 average product value, that is $12,000 in damage costs that never appear on the freight bill.

These are not edge cases. They are the normal operating reality of LTL shipping. The shippers who control LTL costs are the ones who control the data that prevents reclassification, reweigh, and surprise accessorials before the shipment moves.

What is FTL Shipping?

FTL (full truckload) means booking an entire trailer exclusively for your freight. No sharing, no consolidation, no terminal handling. Your shipment goes directly from your dock to the consignee's dock on a single truck.

You fill a trailer, pay for dedicated use, and a driver moves it point-to-point. No intermediate stops, no hub transfers, no other freight on the truck touching yours.

The tradeoff is clear: FTL simplicity requires sufficient volume to justify the cost. A standard dry van trailer offers approximately 2,500 cubic feet of space and can carry up to roughly 44,000 pounds of freight, depending on tractor weight and axle configuration.

For more on full truckload shipping services, see our truckload shipping guide.

FTL works best when:

  • Your shipment exceeds 10,000 lbs or 10 pallets
  • You need faster, direct transit
  • Freight is fragile and can't tolerate multiple handlings
  • You're shipping high-value goods
  • Delivery timing is critical
  • You have a full trailer's worth of freight going to one destination

When FTL Provides Control but Only with Accountability

FTL eliminates the handling risk and accessorial exposure of LTL. But it introduces a different set of risks: carrier communication failures, rate volatility, and load acceptance followed by poor execution.

A truckload carrier who accepts your load at $2,800 and then goes silent for two days creates a different kind of cost. Your customer does not know when to expect delivery. Your warehouse cannot plan receiving. And if the driver is late, nobody told you until the delivery window passed.

FTL delivers its full value only when the carrier is held to communication standards: proactive tracking updates, early delay notification, and rate accountability where the quoted price holds without post-shipment adjustments. Without these standards, FTL's simplicity advantage is undermined by operational uncertainty.

Difference Between LTL and FTL: Detailed Comparison

Cost Structure

LTL Pricing

LTL rates are calculated using multiple variables: weight, freight class, distance, and accessorial services. Freight class (based on density, handling, stowability, and liability) significantly impacts pricing. A dense, easy-to-handle shipment costs less per pound than a light, bulky, fragile one.

LTL also charges accessorial fees for services beyond standard dock-to-dock transport: liftgate ($50-$100), residential delivery ($50-$150), inside delivery ($75-$150), and others. These add up, especially for deliveries to non-commercial locations. For a complete breakdown, see our accessorial charges guide.

FTL Pricing

Truckload rates are simpler: typically a per-mile rate or flat rate for the load. Freight class doesn't factor in because you're paying for the truck, not per-pound capacity. The primary cost drivers are distance, fuel surcharges, and market conditions (carrier availability on your lane).

FTL pricing fluctuates more with market conditions than LTL. During capacity crunches (like Q4 holiday season or weather disruptions), spot truckload rates can spike dramatically. Contract rates provide more stability but require volume commitments.

Cost Comparison Example: The Real World

A furniture manufacturer in North Carolina ships component orders regularly to a distributor in New Jersey. At 5,000 pounds and Class 85, they looked at the obvious choice: LTL at $500-$1,200 depending on carrier, lane, and accessorials. Made sense for light shipments.

Then volume increased. They started moving 12,000 pounds across 10 pallets each week. LTL rates jumped to $1,800-$3,000 per load. FTL stayed flat at $1,500-$2,500 for the same lane. The math reversed overnight.

They consolidated weekly orders into FTL and cut shipping costs by 40%. The crossover point typically falls between 8,000-12,000 pounds or 8-12 pallets, depending on freight class and lane. Always quote both modes for shipments in this range.

Transit Time

LTL Transit: 1-5+ Business Days

LTL freight passes through terminals and hubs, adding time at each transfer point. A 300-mile shipment might take 2 days. Coast-to-coast takes 4-5+ days. Each hub transfer adds roughly half a day to a day of transit.

Day of pickup matters too. Freight picked up Friday afternoon may not move until Monday, effectively adding 2 days to transit. Timing compounds the delay.

FTL Transit: 1-3 Days for Most Lanes

Truckload freight moves directly. No terminals, no sorting, no waiting at distribution hubs. A single driver covers roughly 500 miles per day; team drivers can cover 1,000+ miles. Coast-to-coast FTL transit is typically 3-4 days. LTL takes 5 or more.

If you're restocking a retail location running low on inventory, expediting a production component, or fulfilling a time-sensitive customer order, FTL's speed advantage often justifies the cost premium. Speed has value when inventory is waiting.

Freight Handling

This is where the difference becomes most consequential.

LTL Handling: Multiple Touchpoints

A typical LTL shipment gets handled 4-8+ times between pickup and delivery:

  1. Loaded at origin
  2. Unloaded at origin terminal
  3. Loaded onto linehaul truck
  4. Unloaded at hub (possibly multiple hubs)
  5. Loaded onto next linehaul
  6. Unloaded at destination terminal
  7. Loaded onto delivery truck
  8. Unloaded at destination

Each handling creates opportunity for damage. Forklifts bump pallets. Freight gets stacked. Loads shift during sorting. Professional LTL carriers minimize damage through training and procedures, but the physics of multiple handlings create inherent risk. You can't move pallets eight times without increasing damage probability.

FTL Handling: Minimal Touchpoints

Truckload freight gets handled twice: loaded at origin, unloaded at destination. The trailer stays sealed throughout transit. Nothing gets sorted, transferred, or stacked on top of your freight.

For fragile products, high-value goods, or items with strict handling requirements, FTL's reduced handling advantage is worth the investment.

Flexibility and Frequency

LTL: Maximum Flexibility

LTL carriers pick up shipments daily from most commercial locations. You can ship one pallet today and three pallets tomorrow without commitments. No volume minimums, no trailer requirements, no long-term contracts needed. Contracts can improve rates, but they're optional.

LTL rewards unpredictability. Some weeks you ship daily, others you ship once. That's fine.

FTL: Requires Volume Planning

Booking a truckload requires having enough freight to justify the trailer. If you ship 3 pallets one week and 15 the next, you can't efficiently use FTL every time. Truckload shipping rewards consistency. Regular, predictable volumes on the same lanes work best.

Smart shippers consolidate smaller orders into weekly FTL shipments instead of shipping individual LTL loads daily. This "milk run" approach captures FTL cost advantages by accumulating volume. It takes planning, but the savings justify it.

Tracking and Visibility

LTL Tracking

LTL carriers provide tracking via PRO numbers showing major milestones: picked up, at terminal, in transit, out for delivery, delivered. Some carriers offer more granular GPS-based tracking. Visibility is good overall, though updates may lag during hub transfers.

FTL Tracking

Truckload tracking is increasingly real-time. Many carriers and brokers offer GPS tracking showing exact truck location, ETA updates, and geofence alerts. Because there's one truck and one load, tracking is straightforward and reliable.

When Multi-Stop TL Beats Both Standard LTL and Single-Stop FTL

A home furnishings distributor in Dallas ships to three retail locations in the Houston metro area every week. Each location receives 4-6 pallets. The distributor's options:

Option A: Three separate LTL shipments. Base rate per shipment: $650. Accessorials (appointment, liftgate at one location): $175 average. Total weekly cost: approximately $2,475. Transit: 2-3 days per shipment with separate delivery windows. Damage rate: 3% across the three shipments.

Option B: One FTL to each location. Three separate trucks at $1,800 each. Total weekly cost: $5,400. Each truck runs 70% empty. Massive waste of capacity.

Option C: One multi-stop truckload. Single truck picks up all freight in Dallas, delivers to all three Houston locations in sequence. Total weekly cost: $2,200. Transit: next-day delivery to all three. One truck, one driver, sealed trailer with planned stops. Damage rate: under 1%.

Multi-stop TL saved $275 per week compared to LTL, delivered faster, reduced damage, and eliminated accessorial surprises. Over 52 weeks, that is $14,300 in savings plus avoided damage costs and fewer claims to process.

This is the option most shippers never consider because standard LTL vs. FTL comparisons do not include it.

When to Use LTL vs FTL: Decision Framework

Choose LTL When:

  • Shipment weighs under 10,000 lbs. LTL is almost always more economical for smaller freight.
  • You ship frequently in small quantities. Daily or weekly LTL pickups are easy to arrange.
  • Standard transit times work for you. 2-5 day delivery windows are acceptable.
  • You're cost-focused above all else. LTL's shared-cost model saves money on smaller shipments.
  • Your freight is well-packaged and durable. Products that tolerate multiple handlings without damage.

Choose FTL When:

  • Shipment exceeds 10,000 lbs or 10+ pallets. The cost per pound often beats LTL at this volume.
  • Speed matters to your business. Direct transit is 1-3 days faster than LTL on most lanes.
  • Freight is fragile, high-value, or sensitive. Fewer handlings mean less damage risk.
  • You need guaranteed capacity. FTL ensures a truck is dedicated to your load.
  • Security is important. Sealed trailers with no intermediate access protect sensitive freight.

Quote Both When:

  • Shipments fall between 6-12 pallets or 8,000-15,000 lbs. This is the gray zone where either mode could win on price.
  • You're exploring partial truckload options. Some carriers offer partial TL service that combines benefits of both.
  • Lane economics vary significantly. Certain lanes may favor one mode over the other due to carrier density.

Example: Bulky Furniture Where LTL Base Rate Looks Cheaper but Total Cost Says Otherwise

A mid-size furniture manufacturer ships bedroom sets from their facility in Mississippi to a regional distributor in Tennessee. The shipment is 6 pallets, 4,800 pounds, Class 125 due to the low density and high damage susceptibility of finished furniture.

LTL quote: $1,350 base rate. Looks reasonable. But here is what the base rate does not include:

  • Reclass risk: Furniture ships at Class 125, one of the most frequently disputed classifications. If one pallet is slightly less dense than declared, the entire shipment can be reclassed to Class 150. That is a $200-$400 increase.
  • Damage risk: Finished furniture passing through four terminal handlings has a significantly higher damage rate than dense, durable freight. The manufacturer's historical data shows a 4.5% damage rate on LTL furniture shipments. At $2,200 average claim value, that is $99 in expected damage cost per shipment.
  • Accessorials: The distributor's dock has restricted hours. Appointment fee: $45. One pallet requires redelivery because of a mislabel at the terminal. Redelivery fee: $175.

Actual LTL cost: $1,650-$1,970 once accessorials, reclass risk, and expected damage cost are factored in.

FTL quote: $1,600. Single truck, direct transit, two handling touchpoints, no classification disputes, no terminal damage risk. The furniture arrives in one day instead of three.

Consolidation option: $1,200. The manufacturer combines this shipment with another customer's order heading to the same region. Both shipments share a truck with a single intermediate stop. Fewer handlings than LTL, lower cost than FTL, and the freight avoids terminal sorting entirely.

The LTL base rate was the lowest number on the page. The total cost told a completely different story.

Hybrid Strategies: Getting the Best of Both

Smart shippers don't choose one mode exclusively. They use both strategically to maximize savings across their entire shipping program.

Consolidation Shipping

Accumulate smaller orders going to the same region and ship them together as FTL once a week instead of individual LTL shipments daily. You capture truckload pricing on consolidated volume while still serving multiple destinations. This hybrid approach works especially well for manufacturers with regular shipments to the same distribution centers.

Pool Distribution

Ship FTL to a regional hub, then break the load into individual LTL deliveries for final miles. This captures FTL economics for the long-haul portion and uses LTL's local delivery network for distribution. You get the speed of FTL plus the flexibility of LTL.

Volume LTL / Partial Truckload

For shipments of 6-12 pallets, partial truckload services offer fewer terminal handlings than standard LTL at prices between LTL and FTL. Your freight still shares a trailer, but with fewer co-riders and fewer stops. It's often the sweet spot for mid-sized shipments.

Seasonal Mode-Switching

Some businesses use LTL during low-volume months and switch to FTL during peak seasons when they ship larger quantities. Flexibility between modes keeps costs optimized year-round. Your strategy should follow your business cycle.

Common LTL vs FTL Mistakes

Mistake #1: Defaulting to LTL Without Quoting FTL

Shippers who always use LTL miss savings when volumes justify truckload. If you regularly ship 8+ pallets to the same destination, get FTL quotes. Most shippers never check and overpay for years.

Mistake #2: Booking FTL for Small Shipments

Some shippers assume FTL is always better and overpay for half-empty trailers. A 3,000-pound shipment on a dedicated truck wastes 90% of the trailer capacity you're paying for. This is the opposite mistake but equally expensive.

Mistake #3: Ignoring Accessorial Costs in LTL Comparisons

When comparing LTL and FTL quotes, include all LTL accessorials: liftgate, residential delivery, appointment, inside delivery. These can add $200-$500 to LTL costs, narrowing or eliminating the savings versus FTL. Many shippers get shocked by accessorial bills at delivery time.

Mistake #4: Not Considering Damage Risk

The cheapest option isn't cheapest if your freight arrives damaged. Factor in damage probability, claims processing time, and customer impact when choosing between modes. A 2% damage rate over 100 shipments annually adds up fast.

Mistake #5: Forgetting About Market Conditions

Truckload rates fluctuate with supply and demand. During tight capacity markets, FTL spot rates spike while LTL rates remain more stable. During loose markets, FTL bargains abound. Check current conditions before committing to a mode. Same lanes have very different costs at different times of year.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does LTL vs FTL mean?

LTL stands for less-than-truckload, where multiple shippers share space on the same trailer. FTL stands for full truckload, where one shipper books the entire trailer exclusively. The core difference is whether you're sharing truck space (LTL) or using it all yourself (FTL).

At what weight should I switch from LTL to FTL?

The crossover typically falls between 8,000-12,000 pounds, but it depends on freight class, distance, and lane. Always quote both modes for shipments over 6,000 pounds or 6 pallets. The only way to know for sure is to compare actual quotes. Don't make assumptions based on weight alone.

Is FTL always faster than LTL?

Generally yes, because FTL moves directly without terminal transfers. For short-distance regional shipments, the difference may be minimal (perhaps one day). The speed advantage increases with distance. A 200-mile shipment might save you half a day with FTL. A 1,500-mile shipment could save you 2 days or more.

Can I use both LTL and FTL for my business?

Absolutely. Most experienced shippers use both modes depending on shipment size, urgency, and destination. Using the right mode for each shipment optimizes cost and service across your entire shipping program.

What's the difference between FTL and dedicated trucking?

FTL books a single truckload for a single trip. Dedicated trucking is a longer-term arrangement where a carrier assigns specific trucks and drivers to your account for ongoing, regular transportation needs. Dedicated trucking is recurring FTL with a guaranteed asset and driver continuity.

Is LTL or FTL better for fragile freight?

FTL is better for fragile freight. Period. LTL shipments pass through multiple terminals where they're loaded, unloaded, and sorted. Each transfer creates damage risk. If your freight is fragile and valuable, the FTL premium is often worth the protection. The cost of one damaged shipment often exceeds the FTL premium for several shipments.

Industry-Specific Considerations

Manufacturing and Distribution

Manufacturers often use both modes strategically. Raw materials and large component orders arrive via FTL. Finished goods ship to multiple customers via LTL. During product launches or seasonal peaks, temporary switches from LTL to FTL handle volume surges.

If you're shipping the same products regularly to the same distribution centers, analyze annual lane data. You'll often find that consolidating daily LTL shipments into weekly FTL loads saves 15-25%.

E-Commerce and Direct-to-Consumer

Online retailers face unique challenges because they ship to homes, not warehouses. Most individual orders are too small for even LTL, but inventory replenishment to fulfillment centers often requires freight. The typical pattern: FTL inbound to fulfillment centers (large restocking orders), then parcel or final-mile delivery outbound to consumers.

Food and Beverage

Temperature-controlled products add complexity. Reefer LTL exists but is limited. Fewer carriers offer it, and mixed-temperature loads present challenges. FTL reefer provides dedicated temperature control without compromising for other shippers' freight. For perishable goods, FTL's speed advantage isn't just about convenience — it's about product integrity.

Construction and Building Materials

Heavy materials like lumber, steel, concrete products, and equipment often ship FTL by default due to weight. A standard pallet of concrete pavers can weigh 2,000+ pounds. Six pallets and you're at 12,000 pounds, firmly in FTL territory. Flatbed FTL is common in construction, where oversized items load onto open decks.

Retail Replenishment

Retailers managing store inventory use both modes. Large distribution center transfers move via FTL. Individual store replenishment with smaller, more frequent orders ships LTL. Many large retailers require appointment scheduling and specific delivery windows — FTL predictability helps meet these requirements more consistently.

Calculating Your True Cost: Beyond the Rate

When comparing LTL and FTL quotes, the line-item rate doesn't tell the whole story. Factor in these hidden costs.

Damage Risk Cost

If you ship 100 LTL loads annually with a 2% damage rate, that's 2 damaged shipments per year. If average product value is $5,000 and you recover 60% through claims, you're absorbing $4,000 annually in damage-related costs. FTL's lower damage rate might make the per-shipment premium worthwhile. That's four thousand dollars that never shows up in the freight bill.

Time-in-Transit Cost

Inventory sitting on a truck isn't available for sale. If LTL takes 3 extra days versus FTL, that's 3 days of carrying cost per shipment. For high-velocity, high-value inventory, the carrying cost of slower transit adds up fast. A 2-day advantage compounds to 20 extra days of sellable inventory per year.

Administrative Cost

LTL generates more billing complexity. Class corrections, reweighs, accessorial additions — each requires review and potentially dispute. FTL billing is simpler: a flat rate per load with fewer variables.

Customer Impact Cost

Late deliveries, damaged products, and scheduling uncertainty affect customer relationships. If your customers depend on reliable delivery windows, FTL's consistency has value beyond the freight rate. Lost customers are expensive.

Make the Right Choice

The LTL vs FTL decision is a true-cost decision, not a rate-sheet decision. Match your shipment size to the right mode, factor in variance risk, accessorial exposure, damage probability, and operational predictability, and always compare quotes for borderline shipments.

Most shippers who analyze this decision find opportunities to save 15-30% by switching modes on the right shipments.
